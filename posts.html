01. The certificate said competent. The tribunal disagreed.

PRODUCT 
The certificate said competent.
The tribunal disagreed.

A Head of Compliance rang me last Tuesday.
She had lost a tribunal that morning.

Completion rate: 98%.
Training records: clean.
Handbook: current.

The judge asked one question the file could not answer.
Could anyone in the room actually do it under pressure?

That is the gap most boards never see.
We measure who finished the module.
We do not measure who would freeze in a real conversation.

Pressure Test exists because of that exact phone call.
Email-triggered simulations.
You cannot click next.
You have to speak the words.

The output is not a certificate.
It is a transcript a tribunal would respect.

When the Worker Protection Act lands in October, the All Reasonable Steps test will not care about your LMS.
It will ask whether your people could do it when it counted.

Stop measuring completion.
Start proving competence.

02. A General Counsel asked what we replace.

PRODUCT 
A General Counsel asked me what we replace.

Nothing, I said.
Keep your LMS.
Keep your e-learning library.
Keep your annual refresher.

That landed badly.
He had been pitched by every vendor in the stack for two years.
Each one claiming his existing programme was the problem.

We are not Layer 1, content.
We are not Layer 2, management.
We are Layer 3, testing.

Phishing tests did not replace awareness training.
They proved whether the awareness was working.
Real Talk Studio does the same thing for culture, conduct, and conversation.

You cannot replace policy knowledge with a simulation.
You also cannot prove behavioural competence with a quiz.

Two weeks later he ran a Pressure Test on harassment intervention.
The completion rate had been 100%.
The pass rate, under pressure, was 41%.

He did not need a new training programme.
He needed evidence the current one was working.
It was not.

03. Three years ago today, I lost my job.

REDUNDANCY 
Three years ago today, I lost my job.

Not a layoff with notice.
A Teams call, scripted, fifteen minutes.

I walked into the kitchen and told my wife.
She was holding the baby.
She said, "We'll work it out."

I did not believe her for six months.

Most days I refreshed LinkedIn jobs like a tic.
Some days I just sat in the car at Tesco.
Once I drove past the building I used to work in and felt sick.

Then something quieter happened.
I stopped applying.
I started building.
The first version took eight weekends and a lot of bad coffee.

Today I drove the same route.
I did not feel sick.
I felt nothing.

The redundancy I was scared of for fifteen years turned out to be the door.
I just could not see it while I was holding the handle.

If you got the call this week, I see you.
The door is not the ending.

04. A recruiter called yesterday.

REDUNDANCY 
A recruiter called yesterday.

Big bank, big role, big number.
The kind of number that solves problems.

I thanked him and said no.

He paused.
"Out of interest, what do you do now?"
I told him.
He said, "Mate, I would have killed for that two years ago."

So would I.

The version of me from January 2024 would have taken the call running.
Would have polished his CV.
Would have rehearsed the answers about gaps in employment.

The version of me today did not need a beat to say no.

That is the part of reinvention nobody tells you about.
Not the bit where you launch the new thing.
The bit a year later where the old life calls and you realise you do not want it back.

Identity does not change overnight.
It changes one boring Tuesday at a time.

Until one day a recruiter says the magic number and your mouth says no before your brain does.

LONELINESS

Not the prospect.

In corporate, the calendar tells you you matter.
Stand-ups, syncs, skip-levels, all of it.
You complain about meetings.
You also drown in them.

Out here, the calendar is blank.
You make the noise.
You also live in the quiet.

On a bad day, that quiet sounds like proof you are not real.
On a good day, it sounds like permission.
Most days, honestly, it sounds like both.

I texted my wife and said, "Quiet day."
She said, "Quiet is not the same as broken."

She is right.
But it does not feel like that at 4pm on a Tuesday when nobody has replied.

If you are out here in the silence, I see you.
It is not just you.

06. Today I wanted to quit.

LONELINESS 
Today I wanted to quit.

Not in a dramatic way.
In the quiet way that scares me more.

I sat at my kitchen table at 9am.
Coffee.
Laptop open.
A pipeline that should have made me proud.

And the thought, plain as anything: I do not want to do this today.

Founders do not say this out loud often.
We say "tough day."
We say "grinding."
We say "deep in it."

We do not say "I would quite like to walk away from the thing I built and never open the laptop again."

The honest answer to why is: it is lonely.
I do not have a team to absorb the weight.
I do not have a manager to push back on.
There is just me, the cursor, and a quiet house.

I did not quit.
I made another coffee.
I opened the laptop.
I sent the email.

But the wanting was real.
The wanting is allowed.

07. My son asked why my laptop is always open.

PARENTING 
My son asked why my laptop is always open.

He was not being mean.
He was being a four-year-old who knows what he sees.

I said, "Because daddy is building a thing."
He said, "Is it finished yet?"

I had no good answer.

Sometimes that is true.
Sometimes the truth is messier.

I am doing it because the corporate version of me was unhappy and afraid of being seen failing.
He does not need to know that yet.
He just needs me to close the laptop more often.

So tonight I closed it at 5.30.
We built a Lego ambulance.

The thing I am building will still be there tomorrow.
He will be four for another six months.

09. Compliance training is the second biggest fraud in corporate.

CONTRARIAN 
Compliance training is the second biggest fraud in corporate.

The first is the report it produces.

Every year, a company spends six figures on an LMS.
Every employee clicks through the same module on harassment, bribery, or conduct.
Completion rate hits 98%.

The Head of Compliance walks into the boardroom with a slide that says: We are protected.

Nobody in that room believes the slide.
They smile politely.
They sign off the budget.
The slide goes in the audit pack.

Three months later, the case lands on the GC's desk.
The training records are spotless.
The handbook is current.
The behaviour, somehow, was not.

Everyone is shocked.
Nobody should be.

Watching people click "I agree" was never going to prove they would do the right thing in a real conversation.
We knew this twenty years ago.
We still buy it.

If your annual programme is producing certificates instead of evidence, you do not have a compliance programme.
You have an insurance policy with a hole in it.

10. AI is going to ruin L&D, but not how you think.

CONTRARIAN 
AI is going to ruin L&D, but not how you think.

The fear in the industry is that AI replaces the trainer.
That is not the threat.

The real threat is that AI exposes how little the current product was worth in the first place.

For twenty years, L&D has sold completion as outcome.
Bums on seats.
Modules built.
Refreshes refreshed.

The metric was activity, not change.

You could get away with that when measurement was expensive.
You cannot get away with it now.

A board director can ask an AI tool tomorrow: did our last harassment training change behaviour?
The honest answer, today, is: we do not know.
We never did.

The job of L&D for the next decade is not to fight AI.
It is to face the question we avoided for two decades.

Does this work?

If the answer is no, the budget moves.
Not because AI replaced us.
Because the board finally found out.

#learninganddevelopment #leadership

Most compliance programmes can tell you who completed the module.
None can tell you who would survive an FCA interview tomorrow.

Here is the closest thing to a useful diagnostic I have seen actually work.

Pick three roles that will be in the room if the regulator calls:
- Frontline contact-centre agent.
- Branch manager.
- Customer outcomes analyst.

For each role, write down the question the regulator is most likely to ask.

For Consumer Duty, it is rarely about policy. It is usually one of these:
- "Walk me through the last time a vulnerable customer called."
- "What did you do differently for them?"
- "How would your colleague have handled the same call?"

Now sit with the actual person who does the job.

Ninety seconds. No notes. No script.

Watch what they say. More importantly, watch what they do not say.

The gap between what your programme assumes they know and what they can articulate under mild pressure is your programme's real risk register.

Run that test once a quarter, and you will learn more about your programme than from a year of completion reports.

HEAD OF COMPLIANCE 7/10 
The certificate says yes. The tribunal will ask: how do you know?

I sat with a General Counsel last month who had just lost a harassment case.

The training records were impeccable:
- 100% completion.
- Annual refreshers.
- Signed acknowledgments.

The tribunal still found against them.

The judge's reasoning was simple. Completion proved the employee had seen the policy. It did not prove the employee could apply it on the day it mattered.

That is Allay v Gehlen landing in real cases. "Stale or brief training" is not a reasonable step.

In October, the Worker Protection Act raises the bar again:
- "All reasonable steps" becomes the legal standard.
- Third-party liability is introduced.
- Completion certificates stop counting as evidence.

If your evidence for harassment prevention is a screenshot of an LMS dashboard, you do not have evidence.

You have a screenshot.

A simulation transcript is different. It shows:
- A person was put in a realistic moment.
- They responded to it.
- They were scored against a defined standard.
- They either passed or did not.

That is what a regulator reads. That is what a judge reads.

Stop collecting attendance. Start collecting evidence.

We will not let the person on the other side of the conversation be someone else's learning experience.

The current learning industry calls this acceptable.

It sells modules. Tracks completions. Issues certificates. Sends people into rooms they have never rehearsed in, to handle moments that change other people's lives.

We will not build for that.

Our test for every feature, every simulation, every metric is the same question:

Does this make it better for the person on the other side of the conversation?

If the answer is yes, it ships.
If the answer is no, it does not.

Even if a regulator would be impressed by it.
Even if a buyer would pay for it.
Even if it would be easier.

We are not in the learning business.

We are in the readiness business.

CHRO / HR RISK 7/10 
Three things to test before you trust a first-time manager with a hard conversation.

Most leadership programmes test the first one. Almost none test the other two.

1) Knowledge.
Can they explain what the policy says? What fair process means? When HR needs to be involved?

E-learning will get them here. Multiple choice will get them here. Most programmes stop here.

2) Response under interruption.
Can they hold the structure of the conversation when the other person:
- Cries.
- Gets defensive.
- Says "I want this in writing."
- Pivots to "this is really about something else."

Workshops rarely simulate this honestly. Peers are too kind. Coaches are too rare.

3) Recovery after a misstep.
The manager will say the wrong thing in the first three minutes. The question is whether they notice, and whether they can bring the conversation back.

This is the one almost nobody tests. It is also the one that decides whether the conversation lands as "fair" or as "tribunal claim".

If your programme tests 1 but not 2 and 3, you know what it does well.

Now you also know what to fix this quarter.

#LeadershipDevelopment #ManagerDevelopment
