01. A General Counsel rang me the day after the FCA letter

PRODUCT 
She rang the day after the FCA letter arrived.

A General Counsel I have known for six months.

She wasn't panicking. She was angry.

Not at the regulator. At her own dashboard.

Every completion box was ticked.

Every refresher logged.

Every attestation signed.

None of it answered the question the FCA had asked.

"Show us, behaviourally, that your people can do this under pressure."

She had nothing to send.

Most compliance leaders sit on a stack of inputs.

The regulator, the tribunal and the board only care about outputs.

What did your people actually do when it mattered?

A completion certificate is not evidence.

A timestamped transcript of behaviour under pressure is.

If your June pack is built on completion rates, you do not have a programme.

You have a paper trail.

Worth a 15-minute call if you would rather find this before the regulator does.

Stop sending completion certificates. Start sending transcripts.

09. Most compliance programmes are alibi factories

CONTRARIAN 
Most compliance programmes are alibi factories.

I know how that sounds.

Stay with me.

Walk into any large organisation and ask three questions.

What did you train people on this year?

How many completed it?

What does the dashboard look like?

You will get neat answers to all three.

Now ask a fourth.

What would your people actually do under pressure?

Watch the room go quiet.

That silence is the product most compliance functions have built.

Not behaviour. Not competence. Not culture.

A paper trail you can hand a regulator while you both pretend it counts.

It worked when the standard was "did you do something?"

It does not work when the standard is "all reasonable steps."

A tribunal will not be impressed by your completion rate.

A whistleblower will not be impressed by your e-learning library.

The Heads of Compliance I speak to know all of this.

I have not met one in two years who believes their own dashboard.

That is not their fault.

The category they were sold is the problem.

Stop building alibis. Start building proof.

10. Culture surveys are workplace astrology

CONTRARIAN 
Culture surveys are workplace astrology.

I know HR teams that have run them for ten years.

Beautifully designed. Quarterly cadence. NPS-style dashboards.

Eight figures spent in some organisations.

Tell me what changed.

Most of them cannot.

They can tell you the engagement score moved 3 points.

They cannot tell you what a single manager did differently.

That is not measurement. That is divination.

A regulator will not accept a heatmap.

A tribunal will not accept a happiness score.

A board will not accept "people felt safer this quarter."

What they will ask is: what behaviour changed, and how do you know?

If your answer is a survey, you are looking at perception.

Perception is what people say when they feel watched.

Behaviour is what they do when they think they are not.

You cannot govern an organisation on perception.

You can be sued on behaviour.

Until culture measurement starts grading behaviour, it is decoration.

The category needs to grow up.

#culture #leadership

Cards on the table.

I sat in rooms where senior managers cried about their fathers.

Most of it was not.

You cannot fix a leader by sending them to a two-day off-site.

You cannot fix a culture by sending its leaders to ten of them.

What changes leaders is not workshops.

It's evidence.

A manager who sees the gap between what they think they did and what their team actually experienced changes overnight.

A manager who attends a session on empathy does not.

L&D keeps funding the second one because it photographs better.

Stop training behaviour. Start measuring it.

CONTRARIAN

Compliance teams keep losing to bad behaviour because they own the wrong layer.

They own policy.

They own training.

They own the LMS report.

They do not own competence.

That sits with line managers. And line managers do not have the tools.

The behaviour stays exactly where it was.

A grievance. A leak. A regulator. A whistleblower.

And every executive in the room turns to the Head of Compliance.

Who turns to the slides.

Which say everyone completed everything on time.

That is not a defence.

That is the start of a disclosure schedule.

If you only own the inputs, you carry the blame for the outputs.

Until compliance owns behavioural evidence, this loop repeats.

Stop owning training. Start owning proof.

AI is not going to fix compliance training.

I have sat through fourteen demos in the last six months.
They are all variations of the same idea.
Faster content. Personalised paths. Adaptive quizzes.

The product is impressive.
The premise is wrong.

The premise is that the problem with compliance training is the content.
It is not.
The content has been fine for a decade.

The problem is that completion was never proof of competence.
A faster path to completion is a faster path to the same wrong number.

You can generate a thousand modules a minute.
You still cannot tell the board whether the line manager will hold the conversation when the disclosure lands.

I want the AI in learning to be useful.
But useful means changing the question, not optimising the answer.

Stop asking how fast we can train.
Start asking how we know they can do it.

That is a different product.

10. Compliance training is in the wrong budget

CONTRARIAN 
Compliance training is in the wrong budget.

It has been for twenty years.

We file it under L and D.
L and D is discretionary.
Discretionary is the first line cut when revenue dips.

Then a tribunal lands.
The legal bill is not in L and D.
It is in the legal budget.
The settlement is not in L and D.
It is in the contingency.
The remediation programme the regulator asks for is not in L and D.
It is in the risk budget.

Every downstream cost of bad compliance lives outside the budget that funded the training.
And every Head of Compliance I speak to knows this.

The fix is structural, not creative.
Stop putting prevention spend in a discretionary line.
Move it to the risk budget where the consequences already sit.

The board will notice when the same line covers both.
That is the conversation worth having before the next budget round.

Three things to test before you trust a first-time manager with a hard conversation.

Most leadership programmes test the first one. Almost none test the other two.

1) Knowledge.
Can they explain what the policy says? What fair process means? When HR needs to be involved?

E-learning will get them here. Multiple choice will get them here. Most programmes stop here.

2) Response under interruption.
Can they hold the structure of the conversation when the other person:
- Cries.
- Gets defensive.
- Says "I want this in writing."
- Pivots to "this is really about something else."

Workshops rarely simulate this honestly. Peers are too kind. Coaches are too rare.

3) Recovery after a misstep.
The manager will say the wrong thing in the first three minutes. The question is whether they notice, and whether they can bring the conversation back.

This is the one almost nobody tests. It is also the one that decides whether the conversation lands as "fair" or as "tribunal claim".

If your programme tests 1 but not 2 and 3, you know what it does well.

Now you also know what to fix this quarter.

Anatomy of a harassment disclosure that goes badly in the first three minutes.

I have sat with three HR Directors this year who walked me through their worst-handled cases. The patterns repeat.

Minute one: The receiver tries to soften the room.

- "I'm sure there's been a misunderstanding."

The discloser hears: she is not being believed.

Minute two: The receiver pivots to process.
- "Let me explain what happens next."
- "We will need to involve HR."

The discloser hears: this is now a procedure, not a moment.

Minute three: The receiver tries to control the narrative.
- "Please don't talk to anyone else about this while we investigate."
- "Try not to make eye contact with him in the meantime."

The discloser hears: I am the problem.

Each of these moves comes from good intentions.

In a tribunal transcript, they sound like victim-blaming, evidence-suppression, and gaslighting.

The standard fix is more training. Another module. A revised script.

The actual fix is rehearsal. Run your managers through three realistic disclosures a quarter, with feedback, and watch how fast minute one changes.

That is the only thing I have seen actually move the needle.
