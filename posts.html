03. The Monday after redundancy is the worst day

REDUNDANCY 
The Monday after redundancy is the worst day.

Not the Friday.
The Friday you have adrenaline.
You have phone calls to make.
You have the lawyer's email to read three times.

The Monday is different.

The alarm still goes off at 6.45.
You still reach for the work phone on the bedside table.
For about four seconds you forget.

Then you remember.

There is no inbox.
There is no 9am stand-up.
There is no Slack to open.
The badge on the desk is just plastic now.

I sat on the edge of the bed that Monday for nineteen minutes.
I counted them.

The thing nobody tells you about being made redundant is this.
You do not lose your job.
You lose the shape of your day.
You lose the small things you did not know were holding you up.

The shape came back, slowly.
Different hours.
Different work.
Different rhythm.

If you are on that Monday this week, the shape will return.

It just will not be the one you had.

REDUNDANCY

It turned out to be the most transferable thing I took out of corporate.

The CV-shaped version of you is never the whole version.

Redundancy is just the moment you find out.

Three questions a Head of Compliance can ask before the next audit.

The gap is rarely the policy.
The gap is the gap between the policy and the moment.

That gap is your real risk register.

You do not need a consultant to find it.

Run that exercise once a quarter and you will know more about your programme than any annual report will tell you.

CHRO / HR RISK 7/10 
Three things to test before you trust a first-time manager with a hard conversation.

Most leadership programmes test the first one. Almost none test the other two.

1) Knowledge.
Can they explain what the policy says? What fair process means? When HR needs to be involved?

E-learning will get them here. Multiple choice will get them here. Most programmes stop here.

2) Response under interruption.
Can they hold the structure of the conversation when the other person:
- Cries.
- Gets defensive.
- Says "I want this in writing."
- Pivots to "this is really about something else."

Workshops rarely simulate this honestly. Peers are too kind. Coaches are too rare.

3) Recovery after a misstep.
The manager will say the wrong thing in the first three minutes. The question is whether they notice, and whether they can bring the conversation back.

This is the one almost nobody tests. It is also the one that decides whether the conversation lands as "fair" or as "tribunal claim".

If your programme tests 1 but not 2 and 3, you know what it does well.

A first-time manager froze on Tuesday.

The conversation was a performance review. The team member was a friend before the promotion.

He had read the framework. He had been to the workshop. He had practised in front of the mirror that morning.

Then she said: "Are you serious?"

And he heard himself say: "Look, let's not make this a big thing."

Three weeks later, the same conversation happened. This time with HR in the room.

This is what workshops cannot prepare you for:
- The first time someone you used to manage as a peer pushes back.
- The first time the person on the other side gets tearful.
- The first time you have to say the hard thing and watch what it does to the relationship.

We pretend leadership development teaches this. It doesn't.

It teaches the shape of the conversation. The structure of the framework.

What it cannot teach without realistic, repeated practice is the muscle:
- The muscle to stay in the moment when your instinct is to soften.
- The muscle to deliver the message exactly once and not retreat.
- The muscle to hold the silence after.

If your manager development programme has never simulated push-back under pressure, you are not developing managers.

You are issuing them.

L&D / MANAGER DEV 7/10 
Anatomy of a harassment disclosure that goes badly in the first three minutes.

I have sat with three HR Directors this year who walked me through their worst-handled cases. The patterns repeat.

Minute one: The receiver tries to soften the room.
- "Are you sure you want to do this formally?"
- "I'm sure there's been a misunderstanding."

The discloser hears: she is not being believed.

Minute two: The receiver pivots to process.
- "Let me explain what happens next."
- "We will need to involve HR."

The discloser hears: this is now a procedure, not a moment.

Minute three: The receiver tries to control the narrative.
- "Please don't talk to anyone else about this while we investigate."
- "Try not to make eye contact with him in the meantime."

The discloser hears: I am the problem.

Each of these moves comes from good intentions.

In a tribunal transcript, they sound like victim-blaming, evidence-suppression, and gaslighting.

The standard fix is more training. Another module. A revised script.

The actual fix is rehearsal. Run your managers through three realistic disclosures a quarter, with feedback, and watch how fast minute one changes.

That is the only thing I have seen actually move the needle.

We will keep cutting features that fail this filter, even when buyers ask for them and competitors ship them.
