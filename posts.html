01. The pause in the boardroom

PRODUCT 
I sat in a boardroom last Thursday and watched a Head of Compliance present data she didn't believe in.

98% completion. Green dashboard. Polite nods.

Then a non-exec asked the only question that mattered.

"If a regulator called tomorrow, could you prove they could actually do it under pressure?"

She paused for too long.

That pause is the entire category.

Completion certificates are not behavioural competence. Compliance leaders know it. They present them anyway because nothing else exists in the budget cycle.

The Worker Protection Act lands in October. The standard is "all reasonable steps." Allay v Gehlen already ruled that stale or brief training is not a reasonable step.

Your LMS tells you who logged in. It does not tell you what they would do in the room.

The board does not want another green dashboard. They want evidence a tribunal would respect.

Stop measuring completion. Start proving competence.

#compliance #leadership

02. Phishing tests didn't replace awareness training. They proved it.

PRODUCT 
Nobody replaced their security awareness programme when phishing simulations arrived.

They added them.

Because the awareness programme was necessary, but insufficient.

Then phishing tests proved what everyone suspected. Knowing the policy and acting on it under pressure are different things.

We are watching the same shift happen in compliance now.

Nobody is throwing out their LMS. Nobody is binning the e-learning library.

But the question being asked at audit committee meetings has changed.

It used to be: "What percentage completed the training?"

Now it is: "Can your people do it when the moment arrives?"

Completion is an input. Behaviour under pressure is an outcome.

The Big 4 will sample a few interviews every three years for £100k. That is not assurance. That is theatre dressed in slides.

Continuous, in-context simulations produce something different. Timestamped transcripts. Behavioural scores. An audit trail a regulator would actually read.

That is what Pressure Test was built to produce.

Want to see what an Evidence Locker looks like? DM me.

Stop measuring inputs. Start verifying outcomes.

03. I just rented the title

REDUNDANCY 
Jan 2024: Made redundant from JP Morgan.

May 2024: Sitting in my kitchen at 2pm on a Tuesday, asking my wife if I'd made a terrible mistake.

May 2026: Building a company I would have called impossible eighteen months ago.

I thought losing the role would break me.

What it actually broke was the assumption that the role was me.

For fifteen years I introduced myself with a title and a logo. Both belonged to someone else. I just rented them.

The morning after the call, the title was gone. The logo was gone. The salary was gone.

What was left was the question I'd been avoiding for a decade.

What do you actually want to build?

Most days I still don't have a clean answer. Some days I miss the simplicity of being told what to do.

But I have not once wanted my old badge back.

The redundancy did not end my career. It started the one I was supposed to be having all along.

04. The thing nobody warns you about is the silence

REDUNDANCY 
Your role is no longer required.

I rehearsed those words in my head for years before I ever heard them out loud.

Every restructure rumour. Every quiet team meeting. Every cost programme on the intranet.

When the call finally came it lasted nine minutes.

I made a coffee. I sat at the kitchen table. I stared at the same patch of garden for an hour without moving.

The thing nobody warns you about is the silence.

The calendar invites stop. The Teams notifications stop. The Slack channels go quiet. You stop being copied on everything.

For a week I felt invisible. For a fortnight I felt useless.

Then a strange thing happened. The space stopped feeling like absence and started feeling like room.

Room to think. Room to start. Room to build something I actually wanted to put my name on.

If you are in that silence right now, I am not going to tell you it gets easy.

But it does get yours.

05. The Tesco car park at 11pm

LONELINESS 
I drove to a Tesco car park at 11pm last Tuesday and sat there for twenty minutes.

Engine off. Lights on. Nobody to call.

A deal I had spent three months on had quietly died over email that afternoon.

I did not want to tell my wife yet, because she would worry. I did not want to tell the team yet, because they would worry more.

So I drove.

That is the part of founder life nobody puts in the deck.

The weight is not the hours. The weight is the place to put the weight down.

In a corporate job you walk past someone in the kitchen and say "rough day" and someone says "same."

As a founder you walk past your own reflection in the kettle and that is the whole conversation.

I am not writing this to be brave about it. I am writing this because I read one of these posts last year and finally took a breath.

If you are in that car park tonight, you are not the only one parked.

06. The tab I almost submitted

LONELINESS 
Today I almost applied for a job.

I had the tab open for nine minutes. Head of Learning at a company I respect. £160k. Pension. Bonus. Holiday I would actually take.

I did not hit submit.

But I sat with the open tab long enough to be honest about why it was there.

Three pitches lost last month. A contract delayed for the third time. Two payroll runs that came out of my personal savings.

If you had asked me on Monday whether I love being a founder, I would have answered yes without thinking.

On Wednesday afternoon I would have answered "I don't know."

That is the bit founders rarely write down. The doubt is not a phase you get through. It is a tenant that pays rent in your head every month.

Some weeks you renegotiate the lease. Some weeks you just let it stay.

The tab is closed now. The doubt is still there.

I am not pretending it is gone. I am choosing what to do this afternoon anyway.

07. The third question

PARENTING 
My daughter asked me a question last night and I answered without looking up from my phone.

She asked it again. Same question. Same patient voice.

I still did not look up.

The third time she stopped asking.

I put the phone face down too late.

I have read the books. I know the rules. No phones at the table, presence over productivity, the days are long and the years are short.

I still do this. More often than I want to admit.

The founder myth is that you can be all in on the work and all in on the kids and the trick is just better time management.

The truth is the trick is admitting where the costs fall, and being deliberate about where you refuse to let them fall.

Tonight I am not answering emails after six. Not because I have solved it. Because tonight I owe her a third question that gets answered the first time.

The work will be there at nine.

She might not ask again.

08. Brown hair. A laptop. No body.

PARENTING 
My son drew a picture of me last week.

Brown hair. A laptop. No body.

Just hair and a laptop.

I laughed at first. Then I sat with it for the rest of the evening.

That is the version of me he sees most days. Hunched over the kitchen table, glow on the face, half-listening from the side of the screen.

Founders love to talk about building generational wealth. We talk about it like it is the trump card that justifies the trade.

But the kids do not see the trade. They see the laptop.

They will not remember the round you closed. They will remember the year you were a shape behind a screen.

I am not quitting. I do believe what I am building matters.

But I am going to close the laptop earlier this week. Not as a productivity move. As a portrait revision.

In twenty years, when one of my kids describes me to their kids, I would like the description to include a face.

09. The audience has stopped clapping

CONTRARIAN 
Compliance training is the most expensive piece of theatre in your business.

Six-figure budgets. Annual cycles. Quarterly board reports.

A 30-minute module nobody watches at 1.75x speed in another tab.

And then we put a tick in a box and call it a control.

The legal team knows it does not work. The compliance team knows it does not work. The board half-suspects it does not work.

But everyone has agreed not to say it out loud, because the alternative is admitting the last decade of spend bought a completion rate, not a competence rate.

The Worker Protection Act will end that consensus in October. "All reasonable steps" is not "we ran a module." Allay v Gehlen already said that out loud.

The fix is not more training. The fix is testing the training you already have.

Find out who can actually do the thing under pressure. Fix the gap. Keep the evidence.

That is not a content problem. It is a verification problem.

Stop buying more theatre. Start asking for proof.

#compliance #leadership

10. AI in L&D is mostly a skin

CONTRARIAN 
Most AI in learning is a magic trick.

Same content. Same module. Same completion certificate.

Wrapped in a chatbot that calls you by name and feels modern enough to renew the contract.

That is not transformation. That is a skin.

The interesting thing AI actually does in this space has nothing to do with content creation. It has to do with measurement.

For the first time, you can put a person in a realistic scenario, watch them respond, and score the response against a defined standard.

At scale. Repeatedly. Without a consultant in the room.

That is the bit that breaks the old story.

When the cost of measuring behaviour collapses, the excuse for not measuring it collapses with it.

Boards will start asking. Regulators will start expecting. Tribunals already are.

The next five years in L&D will not be won by the platforms that generated the slickest content.

It will be won by the ones that can prove the content worked.

Stop buying content engines. Start buying evidence engines.

#leadership #compliance
