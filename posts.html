A Head of Compliance told me last week she has nightmares about live audit interviews.

Not the document review.
Not the policy walk-through.

The bit where a 22-year-old in the contact centre is sat across from an FCA examiner who asks: "Walk me through the last time a vulnerable customer called you."

Her programme is solid:
- Policies tight.
- Annual refreshers done.
- Consumer Duty module at 98% completion.

But she has no way to know what that 22-year-old will actually say in the room.

That is the gap. And it is the gap regulators have started walking through.

The shift from "did you train them?" to "can your people do it?" is not theoretical.

- Six confirmed Consumer Duty investigations as of January.
- Four thematic reviews underway.
- Every one ends in a live interview.

Your LMS will not be in that room. Your e-learning library will not be in that room.

The person and the moment will be in that room.

The only credible evidence the programme works is a transcript of a realistic conversation.

HEAD OF COMPLIANCE 7/10 
The certificate says yes. The tribunal will ask: how do you know?

I sat with a General Counsel last month who had just lost a harassment case.

The training records were impeccable:
- 100% completion.
- Annual refreshers.
- Signed acknowledgments.

The tribunal still found against them.

The judge's reasoning was simple. Completion proved the employee had seen the policy. It did not prove the employee could apply it on the day it mattered.

That is Allay v Gehlen landing in real cases. "Stale or brief training" is not a reasonable step.

In October, the Worker Protection Act raises the bar again:
- "All reasonable steps" becomes the legal standard.
- Third-party liability is introduced.
- Completion certificates stop counting as evidence.

If your evidence for harassment prevention is a screenshot of an LMS dashboard, you do not have evidence.

You have a screenshot.

A simulation transcript is different. It shows:
- A person was put in a realistic moment.
- They responded to it.
- They were scored against a defined standard.
- They either passed or did not.

That is what a regulator reads. That is what a judge reads.

Stop collecting attendance. Start collecting evidence.

#compliance #WorkerProtectionAct
Critique: Sharpest hook of the five. "You have a screenshot" is the kind of line people screenshot back. Real legal precedent (Allay v Gehlen) earns credibility. Hard parallel close. Travels beyond Compliance into Legal and Risk.

HEAD OF COMPLIANCE 8/10 
A customer crossed a line. Nobody on the team knew what to do.

Set the scene:
- Twenty-two-year-old on the desk.
- Comment from a regular.
- Mid-shift, three other customers in earshot.

She froze. The duty manager smiled it off. The shift ended.

Two weeks later, the same customer. Same comment. Different employee.

This time it escalated:
- A grievance on Monday.
- A tribunal claim within the month.
- A Sunday Times paragraph by quarter end.

The training was in place. Harassment module completed in March. Signed off. Filed.

But nobody had ever rehearsed the moment.

From October, the Worker Protection Act says you are liable for harassment by customers, clients and suppliers, unless you can show all reasonable steps were taken.

"All reasonable steps" is a behavioural test, not a procedural one.

- Showing the policy was issued is not enough.
- Showing the module was completed is not enough.
- You need to show your people knew what to do, and had practised doing it, before the day it mattered.

Workshops do not give you that evidence. E-learning does not give you that evidence.

Practice does.

#WorkerProtectionAct #HR
Critique: Strong escalation (grievance → tribunal → press). Worker Protection Act framing is timely. "Sunday Times paragraph" leans cinematic — works if you've witnessed something close to this; feels invented otherwise. Replace with a more grounded consequence if it doesn't ring true.

CHRO / HR RISK 7/10 
A manager I spoke to last month had a disclosure land on her desk on a Friday at 4.50pm.

She had:
- Thirty minutes before her train.
- No notes.
- Two children to collect.

She did the worst version of the right thing.

She listened. Then she said: "Let me get back to you on Monday."

By Tuesday it was a grievance.
By Thursday it was a complaint to HR about how the grievance had been handled.

From April this year, that disclosure is also protected under whistleblowing law.

Mishandle it, and you are not just exposed on the original behaviour.
You are exposed on the response.

Most managers have never been taught what to say in the first ninety seconds of a disclosure.

Not because the policy is hidden. Because nobody practises with them before the real one arrives.

- A scripted reply is not training.
- A workshop two years ago is not preparation.
- A "we take this very seriously" line under pressure is not a defence.

The only thing that builds the muscle is a realistic scenario, run before the real one lands.

#HR #whistleblowing
Critique: "She did the worst version of the right thing" is a screenshot line. The manager is sympathetic, the failure is recognisable, the regulatory hook (whistleblowing law from April 2026) is fresh. Most relatable post of the five — likely to travel outside the HR niche.

CHRO / HR RISK 8/10 
The first difficult conversation most managers ever have is the real one.

Pick any of these:
- The performance warning.
- The grievance response.
- The return-to-work after a long absence.

They have read about it.
They have sat through a workshop.
They may have done a polite role-play with a colleague who agreed not to make it weird.

Then it lands for real:
- Someone cries.
- Someone gets defensive.
- Someone says "I don't accept that, and I want this in writing."

The training does not transfer because the training was never put under pressure.

Heads of L&D know this. It is the Kirkpatrick problem that has dogged the function for forty years.

Knowledge moves. Behaviour does not.

The fix is not more theory. The fix is realistic practice, at scale, with feedback, before the live conversation.

- Not a peer who is too kind.
- Not a coach you can only afford for the top fifty.
- Not a workshop in March that has to carry the manager through November.

A scenario that pushes back, surprises them, and gives them a transcript they can review afterwards.

That is finally how a manager can train.

#LeadershipDevelopment #ManagerDevelopment
